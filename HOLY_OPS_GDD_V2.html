Section 1.0

Executive Summary

HOLY_OPS.EXE is a single-player trading game disguised as a medieval relic auction. The operator runs a clandestine relic exchange terminal, buying sacred bones and teeth at auction, holding them as the market fluctuates, and selling them for profit while attempting to complete saint collections before time runs out.

The game occupies a unique intersection: Bloomberg Terminal meets Canterbury Tales. It is an information-dense, aesthetically precise experience about reading a market, exploiting edges, and deciding when a relic is worth more as capital and when it is worth more as devotion.

The tension between trading and collecting, between profit and devotion, is the game.

Design Pillars

Three principles govern the design. First, the terminal aesthetic is not a skin. It is the gameplay surface. The BSP grid is a living heat map where lot size reflects market activity, the scrolling feed is a skill-gated intelligence stream, and the portfolio view creates the emotional texture of real trading. Second, the medieval relic setting provides something no financial theme can: emotional attachment. A treasury bond is a number; the transverberated heart of St. Teresa is a story. Third, the heresy system creates learnable patterns that reward deep engagement. The operator who has internalized that Cathari events depress Catherine and Teresa prices, who reads the terminal feed for early signals, who watches rival collectors for positioning data, is not clicking buttons. They are reading a market.

Genre Classification

Real-time strategy / auction simulator / portfolio management. Single-player. Session-based (15-25 minutes). Web platform (browser-native).

Section 2.0

Core Loop

Each session is structured as a series of Market Days. The day follows a four-phase cycle that creates a rhythm of tension and relief, chaos and contemplation.

Market Day Cycle
BRIEFING 15s Scan feed for signals. Form a plan.
OPEN MARKET 3m Real-time auctions. Buy, sell, hold.
CLOSING BELL 30s Final scramble. Lots expire rapidly.
SETTLEMENT -- Review. Enshrine. Plan tomorrow.

2.1 Briefing Phase

Fifteen seconds. The terminal feed populates with tomorrow's intelligence: demand signals, heresy warnings, rival collector movements, lot previews. The operator scans for actionable patterns. This is not a pause — it is the moment before the doors open. The pre-sale viewing. It is where the excitement begins.

2.2 Open Market

Three minutes of live trading. Lots spawn continuously on the BSP grid. AI bidders compete in real time. Prices fluctuate. Heresy events fire and disrupt the market, creating four-second tribunal delays where AI bidding freezes and the operator can bid unopposed. The operator buys relics at auction when they are underpriced and sells them on the open market when they are overpriced.

2.3 Closing Bell and Settlement

A thirty-second closing bell accelerates the final lot expirations. Then the market closes and settlement begins. Settlement is untimed. The operator sees a market summary: what prices moved, which AI collectors acquired what, what the forecast projects. Then the enshrine decision.

2.4 The Enshrine Decision

Enshrining permanently locks a relic into the saint collection. It cannot be traded, sold, or recovered. Every enshrine reduces liquid capital and trading capacity. But it advances the saint completion that determines the final score. The tension is irreversible: every relic in the portfolio is simultaneously an investment and a potential piece of a sacred collection. Sell it for profit, or enshrine it for progress.

Design Intent

The enshrine animation should evoke placement in a reliquary. The relic moves from a trading card to a sacred object. The visual register shifts from commerce to devotion. The sound is stone sliding into place. This moment is what all the trading was for.

Section 3.0

Market System

3.1 Price Discovery Model

Relic prices follow an Ornstein-Uhlenbeck mean-reverting process. Each relic has an equilibrium price (mu) determined by its intrinsic attributes. Prices drift toward this equilibrium over time but are subject to random fluctuations (sigma) and event-driven shocks. The model updates every five seconds.

The mean-reversion creates a fundamental truth: prices that are far from equilibrium tend to correct. This is the edge that rewards patient, informed operators. A relic trading 30% below its equilibrium after a heresy event is a buying opportunity. A relic trading 40% above its equilibrium due to a demand surge is a selling opportunity.

Transparency Principle

Every price movement must have a visible cause in the terminal feed. The operator should never feel cheated by an opaque formula. When the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck model causes a price to spike, the feed explains it through provenance narrative or market event.

Section 5.0

Section 6.0

Market Events

6.1 Heresy System

Five historical heterodox sects disrupt the market in thematically grounded ways. Each sect has a specific impact on specific saints, creating a learnable mapping that rewards deep engagement. Heresy events are the game's equivalent of earnings reports in stock trading. They move the market. The skilled operator reads the sect name and instantly knows which saints are affected.

When a heresy event fires, AI bidding freezes for four seconds (the Tribunal Delay). This is the operator's window to act on information before the market can react.

Sect Theology Primary Targets Market Effect
Cathari Rejected material relics as the work of the Demiurge Catherine, Teresa Depresses prices. Broad market downturn with concentrated effect on female saints.
Flagellants Introduced counterfeit penitential relics Sebastian, Francis Supply confusion. Spikes prices on martyrdom and stigmata relics as buyers seek authenticated pieces.
Waldensians Targeted commercialization of the sacred High-value lots Disrupts expensive relics. Class I prices drop. Class III unaffected. Progressive taxation on value.
Bogomils Rejected the cross and physical devotion Bartholomew, Ambrose Depresses Passion-adjacent relics. Creates buying window for body-part-heavy saints.
Gnostic Order Claimed secret knowledge invalidated public relics All saints (mild) Mild universal depression. Authentication challenges increase. Uncertainty premium rises.

6.2 Macro Events

Beyond heresy, the market responds to large-scale historical events that create structural shifts in supply and demand.

Event Effect Duration
Papal Bull Demand surge for specific saint. Prices spike across all relic classes. 120-180s
Crusade Haul Supply flood. Many new lots at depressed opening prices. 90-150s
Monastery Dissolution Fire sale. Relics from one saint enter market below equilibrium. 60-120s
Pilgrimage Wave Demand surge for popular saints. Teresa, Francis prices increase. 90-120s
Estate Sale Mixed lot dump from a deceased collector. Randomized saint, below market. 45-90s
Papal Subsidy Emergency capital injection of 100 credits. Prevents operator bankruptcy. Instant
Section 7.0

Operator Interface

The operator interface is a three-column command center built on a BSP (Binary Space Partitioning) grid layout. Information density is a feature, not a side effect. The interface is modeled on financial trading terminals: every element serves a functional purpose. There is no decoration.

BSP Grid Behavior

The market grid uses Binary Space Partitioning to dynamically size auction cells based on their weight. Lots the operator has bid on stay large and visible (+1.0 weight). Lots nearing expiry grow to signal urgency (+0.5 weight under 15 seconds). Active lots with many bids expand to reflect market heat. The grid is a living heat map: the operator can read market sentiment from the shape of the layout before parsing a single number.

Terminal Feed

The feed is not decoration. It is the primary skill surface. Market transactions, heresy reports, AI collector movements, demand events, and authentication results scroll in real time. The operator who reads the feed trades better than the operator who ignores it. Attention allocation is the core skill.

Margin Trading

Advanced operators can borrow against their portfolio at 3% per minute. This enables leveraged positions when the operator has high conviction on a price movement, but creates significant downside risk if the trade moves against them. Margin trading is not surfaced in the tutorial. It is discovered through the terminal feed.

Section 9.0

Session Structure

Market Days

A session consists of 12 market days. Each day runs the Briefing-Market-Bell-Settlement cycle. Total session length is approximately 15-25 minutes depending on settlement deliberation time. The session ends after the twelfth settlement, regardless of the operator's capital or completion status.

Scoring Formula

The final score combines collection progress, financial performance, and trading skill into a single composite number.

Scoring Calculation
COLLECTION Saints completed x 1000 (primary)
PROGRESS Relics enshrined x 50 (partial credit)
NET WORTH Final cash + portfolio value (tiebreaker)
EFFICIENCY Profitable trades / total trades (multiplier: 0.8x-1.2x)
----------------------------------------------
FINAL SCORE (Collection + Progress + Net Worth) x Efficiency

Grade Thresholds

Grade Requirement Description
S 3+ saints completed, net worth > starting capital Master operator. Exceptional market reading and capital management.
A 2 saints completed Accomplished. Strong trading instincts. Good heresy exploitation.
B 1 saint completed Competent. Completed a primary objective. Room for trading improvement.
C 50%+ progress on at least one saint, positive net worth Developing. Understanding the systems but not yet exploiting them.
D Some relics enshrined, capital remaining Novice. Learning the interface. Not yet reading the market.
F Bankruptcy or no enshrined relics Failed operation. The Commission has noted your performance.

End States

The session can end in three ways: completion of the twelfth market day (standard), operator bankruptcy (capital reaches zero with no sellable holdings), or early termination by the operator. In all cases, the final score is calculated and the grade assigned. There is no win or lose state — only a grade that reflects the operator's performance across the full session.

Engine Design

All game logic resides in pure functions. The engine module exports tick, placeBid, aiBids, and spawnLot as functions that take the current GameState and return a new GameState. No side effects. No mutation. This architecture enables deterministic replay, straightforward testing, and clean separation between game logic and rendering.

Engine Interface
tick(state: GameState) -> GameState // 1s clock cycle
placeBid(state, lotId, amount) -> GameState
aiBids(state: GameState) -> GameState // 3s AI cycle
spawnLot(state: GameState) -> GameState // 5s spawn cycle
sellRelic(state, relicId) -> GameState // instant market sell
enshrine(state, relicId) -> GameState // permanent lock

State Management

Game state is a single useState<GameState> in the root component, updated via pure functions from the engine module. Memoized components (GridCell, LotTicker, SaintTracker, TerminalFeed, Collection) prevent unnecessary re-renders during the one-second tick cycle. CSS-only animations handle all visual effects.

Section 11.0

Expansion Concepts

PRE-DECISIONAL DRAFT — NOT FOR IMPLEMENTATION

The following concepts represent potential expansion vectors for the HOLY_OPS exchange platform. Each has been assessed for thematic coherence, mechanical depth, and integration feasibility. None have been approved for development. This section serves as a research index for future design cycles.

11.1 Souls Futures Market

A derivatives panel enabling forward contracts on saint asset classes. Operators would purchase futures contracts speculating on the direction of a saint's relic prices over the next 3-5 market days. The mechanic draws from the historical indulgence economy, where the Church effectively sold futures contracts on salvation — paying now for a reduction in future purgatorial time. A souls futures panel would display a yield curve per saint, with contract prices reflecting aggregate market expectations. The operator who correctly reads upcoming heresy patterns and demand events could profit from futures positions without ever touching the underlying relics.

11.2 Doctrine Deployment

An active market manipulation mechanic. The operator could expend Obols to deploy doctrinal communications — papal bulls, excommunication threats, miracle proclamations — that influence AI participant behavior. Deploy a bull praising Teresa and Pilgrim demand for Teresa relics surges. Issue an excommunication targeting the Borgia collector and their bidding pauses for two market days. The mechanic is grounded in the historical reality of the Church's propaganda apparatus: papal communications were instruments of economic warfare, capable of redirecting pilgrim traffic, invalidating rival claims, and creating or destroying market demand at will.

11.3 Currency Exchange / Obol Markets

The Obol (◈) — named for the coin placed in the mouths of the dead as payment to Charon for passage across the River Styx — is currently the sole unit of account. An expansion could introduce historical currency pairs: the Venetian ducat, the Florentine florin, the Byzantine bezant, the livre tournois. Provenance origin would determine pricing currency. A Venetian-provenance relic would be priced in ducats; a French-provenance relic in livres. Exchange rates would fluctuate based on geopolitical events in the terminal feed, creating a forex layer atop the relic market. The operator who notices a bezant devaluation could buy Constantinople-provenance relics cheaply before the exchange rate corrects.

11.4 Indulgence Index

A parallel market tracking the price and availability of indulgences — the Church's mechanism for remitting temporal punishment due for sins. When indulgences are cheap and plentiful, relic demand contracts: why purchase a saint's bone when you can buy forgiveness directly? When indulgences are restricted — as during reform periods or papal succession crises — relic demand surges as the faithful seek alternative channels of grace. The Indulgence Index would function as a leading macro indicator, displayed as a graph in the terminal interface. Its movements would forecast broad market direction, rewarding operators who monitor the spiritual economy alongside the material one.

11.5 Pilgrimage Route Intelligence

A graph-based visualization of pilgrimage traffic patterns across major European routes: the Camino de Santiago, the Via Francigena, the Canterbury Road. Routes would illuminate based on simulated traveler volume, functioning as a leading indicator for saint-specific demand surges. When the Camino lights up, Francis and James relics appreciate. When the Via Francigena activates, Roman saints (Sebastian, Bartholomew) see increased interest. The panel would provide spatial-temporal intelligence that complements the text-based terminal feed, giving the operator a second information channel to read.

11.6 Schism Risk Index

A macro volatility indicator tracking the probability of a major ecclesiastical schism — modeled on the Great Western Schism of 1378-1417, when competing papal claimants divided European allegiance and disrupted every institution of the Church. As the Schism Risk Index rises, all market volatility increases. At critical thresholds, specific events fire: rival authentication authorities emerge, competing price feeds diverge, AI participants split into factions. A full schism event could temporarily split the exchange into two competing markets with different prices for the same relics, creating arbitrage opportunities for operators nimble enough to trade across the divide.

The Ten Commandments of PCSAI Design

  1. No gradients. Flat fills only. Light comes from data, not decoration.
  2. Two typefaces only. DM Sans (corporate) and IBM Plex Mono (terminal). No exceptions.
  3. Monospaced numbers always. All financial data uses tabular-nums in IBM Plex Mono.
  4. Semantic color only. Green means gain. Red means loss. Amber means caution. Never decorative.
  5. Borders, not shadows. Depth is indicated by 1px solid lines, never box-shadow.
  6. Uppercase sparingly. Labels and headers only. Body text is sentence case.
  7. Terminal surfaces are sacred. Nothing corporate (rounded corners, soft shadows) enters the terminal register.
  8. Data density over whitespace. Bloomberg, not Apple. Show more, not less.
  9. Animate with purpose. Pulse for live data. Blink for warnings. Never for decoration.
  10. The hole-punch dots are non-negotiable. The left-margin dots ground every page in physical bureaucracy.
Appendix C RESEARCH

Relic Market Research

Document Status

Primary research into the historical economics of medieval relic trading. Findings directly informed the pricing model, relic classification system, and provenance mechanics in the current design.

“The medieval relic economy was not irrational — it was a sophisticated market where provenance, miracles, and institutional backing created measurable price differentiation.”

Key Findings

  • Three axes of valuation: Proximity to the saint (contact vs. representative), miracle attestation count, and institutional authentication.
  • Provenance chains functioned like modern certificates of authenticity. A relic with a documented chain from Rome commanded 3–5x the price of an undocumented equivalent.
  • Fraud was endemic — multiple heads of John the Baptist existed simultaneously. Markets adapted by pricing authentication risk directly.
  • Class I relics (body parts) were categorically more valuable than Class II (personal effects) or Class III (contact relics), often by an order of magnitude.
  • Institutional demand from cathedral chapters and monasteries created reliable floor prices. Private collectors added volatility.

Recommended Price Ranges by Class

Relic ClassDescriptionBase RangeAuthenticated Range
Class IBody parts, bones, blood◆ 800–1,400◆ 1,200–2,200
Class IIPersonal effects, clothing◆ 300–700◆ 500–1,000
Class IIIObjects touched to relics◆ 80–250◆ 150–400

Design Implications

  • Provenance attestation should be a visible data field on each lot, directly affecting fair value
  • Authentication risk creates natural price volatility — heresy events can revoke authentication
  • Institutional vs. private demand maps cleanly onto AI collector personalities
  • The class system provides intuitive risk tiers for new players
Appendix D FUTURE-FACING

Trading Terminal Research

Document Status

UX analysis of professional trading terminals (Bloomberg, Refinitiv, TradingView) to inform the HOLY_OPS interface evolution. These patterns represent the aspirational direction for post-v1 keyboard-driven workflow.

Key Findings

  • Bloomberg’s Launchpad model: Users compose custom dashboards from standard panel types. Each panel has a consistent title bar, data density, and keyboard shortcut layer.
  • Keyboard-first workflow: Professional traders rarely use mice. All critical actions are bound to function keys and shortcut chords. The keyboard is the primary input device.
  • Information density over simplicity: Bloomberg displays 200+ data points on a single screen. Novice-friendliness is sacrificed for expert efficiency. Our game should trend this direction.
  • Color discipline: Red/green for direction, amber for alerts, white for neutral data. Bloomberg uses no decorative color. We follow the same principle.
  • Panel composition: Users can tile, stack, and link panels. A click in one panel filters data in linked panels. This cross-referencing model maps to our lot/portfolio/market panels.

Appendix E CORE VISION

Trading Redesign

Document Status

This is the central design document for the v1.0 trading system. It defines the sell-vs-enshrine mechanic, the 4-phase market day, and the scoring model. All other design decisions flow from this document.

“The core tension is not buy-vs-pass. It is sell-vs-enshrine — do you cash out for immediate gain, or lock the relic into your permanent collection for scoring power?”

The Sell-vs-Enshrine Decision

When a player wins an auction, the relic enters their portfolio (liquid inventory). From the portfolio, the player has two options:

  • Sell: List the relic on the open market at current market price. Generates immediate currency. The relic is gone.
  • Enshrine: Lock the relic into the permanent collection. No currency return. The relic contributes to the final devotion score via set bonuses, rarity multipliers, and provenance chains.

4-Phase Market Day

PhaseDurationActivityMarket Behavior
Dawn60sNew lots revealed, market opensLow volatility, discovery pricing
Midday90sPeak auction activityHigh volume, competitive bidding
Vespers60sFinal auctions, enshrine windowRising volatility, last-chance energy
Compline30sMarket closed, scoringNo trading, devotion calculated

Scoring Formula

ComponentFormulaMax Contribution
Base DevotionSum of enshrined relic devotion values~40%
Set BonusComplete saint set × 1.5 multiplier~25%
Provenance ChainLinked authentication bonuses~15%
Rarity PremiumClass I: 3x, Class II: 1.5x, Class III: 1x~10%
Treasury BonusRemaining currency × 0.1~10%

Design Goals

  • Every auction win should trigger a meaningful decision (sell or enshrine)
  • Currency is a tool, not a score — hoarding cash yields diminishing returns
  • The 4-phase day creates natural pacing with escalating urgency
  • AI collectors create price pressure that makes sell timing matter
  • Set collection creates long-term strategy across multiple market days
Appendix F SYNTHESIS

Expert Roundtable Synthesis

Document Status

Synthesis of a multi-perspective design roundtable covering game economy, UX architecture, and market simulation. Eight consensus points emerged alongside key disagreements documented below.

“HOLY_OPS sits at the intersection of incremental-game dopamine and financial-terminal competence fantasy — the trick is making the complexity feel earned rather than imposed.”

Eight Consensus Points

  1. Sell-vs-enshrine is the right core tension. It creates an irreversible, emotionally weighted decision at the heart of every auction win.
  2. The 3-column layout is correct. Ticker (left), grid (center), sidebar (right) mirrors professional terminal conventions and should not change.
  3. AI collectors need visible personalities. Named agents with predictable-but-exploitable bidding patterns make the market feel alive.
  4. Heresy events should differentiate by sect. Iconoclast, Gnostic, and Arian heresies should produce mechanically distinct market disruptions.
  5. Starting currency should decrease from v0.1. ◆3,000 forces tighter resource management than ◆5,000 and makes early trades consequential.
  6. The terminal aesthetic is a competitive advantage. No other casual game looks like a Bloomberg terminal. Lean into it.
  7. Sound design is critical. Terminal beeps, ticker sounds, and heresy alarms create the “ops room” feeling that screenshots cannot convey.
  8. Session length should target 8–12 minutes. Long enough for strategy, short enough for mobile sessions.

Appendix G CURRENT

Gameplay Research

Document Status

Friction analysis and parameter tuning findings from playtesting the v0.1 build. These findings directly informed the parameter changes documented in Appendix A and the redesign in Appendix E.

Friction Analysis — V0.1

  • Bid feedback was too slow. Players did not know if their bid was winning or losing until the lot resolved. Fix: real-time bid status indicators (winning/outbid/leading).
  • Too many simultaneous lots. 18 initial lots overwhelmed new players. Most ignored 60%+ of the grid. Fix: reduce to 12, introduce lots progressively.
  • No sell mechanic meant no recovery. A bad early bid was permanently punishing. Players felt trapped. Fix: portfolio + sell system.
  • Heresy events were confusing. Players did not understand why their screen froze. Fix: clear pre-event warning, sect identification, and countdown timer.
  • End state was abrupt. Running out of money ended the game instantly. Fix: market day phases with defined closing sequence.
  • Collection had no payoff. Winning relics had no mechanical benefit. Fix: devotion scoring, set bonuses, enshrine system.

Appendix H FUTURE-FACING

Futures Panel Research

Document Status

Exploration of six expansion concepts that extend the HOLY_OPS economy beyond the core auction loop. These are post-v1 features documented here for reference and prioritization.

Expansion Concepts

1. Souls Futures Market

Players trade futures contracts on saint devotion indices. A “St. Catherine Futures” contract pays out if Catherine-tagged relics appreciate during the session. Creates a meta-layer of speculation on top of the auction.

2. Doctrine Deployment

Players spend influence points to enact temporary market rules — banning a relic class, boosting a saint’s value, or triggering a targeted heresy. A governance mechanic that makes players complicit in market manipulation.

3. Obol Currency Exchange

Multiple in-game currencies (Obols, Denarii, Florins) with fluctuating exchange rates. Players must convert between currencies to bid on regionally-tagged lots, adding a forex layer to the trading loop.

4. Indulgence Index

A synthetic index tracking overall market piety. High piety = stable prices, low piety = volatile markets. Players can buy index-linked instruments to hedge against market-wide heresy events.

5. Pilgrimage Route Intelligence

A map panel showing relic supply chains from origin sites to market. Disruptions along routes (banditry, plague, schism) reduce supply and spike prices for affected relics. Players with route intelligence can front-run supply shocks.

6. Schism Risk Index

A real-time gauge measuring the probability of a market-splitting schism event. When the index hits critical levels, the market bifurcates into competing exchanges with different price feeds. Players must choose a side.
